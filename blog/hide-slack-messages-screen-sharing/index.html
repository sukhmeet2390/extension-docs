How to Hide Slack Messages During Screen Sharing: 6 Practical Options

2026-07-20
#slack#screen sharing#privacy#remote work#productivity

You’ve probably had this moment: you’re sharing your screen, talking through a project update, and then you notice Slack sitting there with a private message preview, a sensitive channel name, or a thread you definitely didn’t mean to show the whole room.

If you need a fix fast, Privacy Blur for Slack is the most practical option because it lets you keep Slack open while hiding the parts you don’t want coworkers or clients to see. But it’s not the only way to reduce the risk.

This guide walks through the best ways to hide Slack messages during screen sharing, ranked by how well they protect your privacy without slowing down your meeting. Some methods are perfect if Slack needs to stay visible. Others work better if you just want the safest possible setup before you click Share.

Why Slack doesn’t have a built-in solution

Slack gives you lots of control over notifications, status, and window layout, but it still doesn’t offer a true presenter mode that hides message content while you share your screen. That means there is no native Slack setting that blurs existing chats, masks channel names, or hides media previews in the main interface.

Slack’s own Do Not Disturb tools help with new interruptions, not existing content already visible on your screen. So if your sidebar includes confidential channels, your DMs contain employee information, or a thread has customer details in plain view, you need a workaround before the meeting starts.

The good news: you don’t need to overhaul your workflow. In most cases, one of the six options below is enough.

1. Privacy Blur for Slack — Best if Slack needs to stay on screen

Install Privacy Blur for Slack

If Slack actually needs to be visible during the meeting, this is the best option because it solves the real problem instead of working around it. Privacy Blur for Slack works on app.slack.com in Chrome and lets you blur the parts of Slack that usually cause embarrassment during screen sharing: messages, names, channels, and media.

The big advantage is control. Instead of blurring the entire page, you can choose from 6 independent blur zones and keep the rest of Slack usable. Need to glance at a message without turning protection off completely? You can hover to reveal specific content when you need it. There’s also an Alt+Shift+B shortcut for quick toggling before a call starts.

It runs 100% client-side with no data collection, so your Slack data stays in your browser, and the free plan is enough for most people. The Pro plan ($3/month) adds persistence and rules if you want your preferred blur setup to stick automatically.

How to use it:

  1. Install the extension from the Chrome Web Store.
  2. Open Slack Web in Chrome.
  3. Turn on the blur zones you want before your meeting starts.
  4. Hover to reveal only what you need while sharing.

This is the most complete choice for managers, recruiters, founders, support teams, and anyone who has to keep Slack open during live calls.

2. Share only the specific window or tab you’re presenting

If you don’t actually need Slack on screen, the safest method is simply not to share it. Zoom, Google Meet, and Microsoft Teams all let you share a specific window instead of your full desktop, and Meet also lets you share a single browser tab.

That means you can present your slide deck, browser tab, design file, or spreadsheet while Slack stays on another part of your computer that viewers never see. For many people, this is the easiest zero-install fix.

How to use it:

  1. Open only the file, browser tab, or app you plan to present.
  2. Click Share or Present in your meeting tool.
  3. Choose Window or Tab instead of Entire Screen.
  4. Double-check the preview before confirming.

This works especially well for status updates, demos, and training sessions where Slack is not part of the presentation. The limitation is obvious: if you need to switch into Slack mid-meeting, you’ll either need to stop sharing, reshare a different window, or risk showing it anyway. Great safety, less flexibility.

3. Turn on Slack Do Not Disturb or pause notifications

If your main fear is surprise popups, Slack’s built-in Do Not Disturb feature is worth using every time you present. It pauses desktop alerts, mobile notifications, and other interruptions for a set amount of time, which immediately lowers the odds of a private message flashing onto your screen at the worst moment.

How to use it:

  1. In Slack, click your profile picture.
  2. Open Notifications.
  3. Choose a pause length such as 30 minutes, 1 hour, or a custom time.
  4. Start your meeting once the snooze icon appears.

This is quick, official, and free. It also helps if you’re presenting in front of clients, leadership, or a large internal meeting where even a harmless DM preview would feel distracting.

The downside: Do Not Disturb does not hide existing conversations already open in Slack. Your channel list, message history, names, and media are still visible if Slack itself is on screen. Think of this as a backup layer, not a complete privacy solution. It’s best paired with window-only sharing or a blur tool.

4. Put Slack on a second virtual desktop

Virtual desktops are a simple middle ground between “close Slack completely” and “leave Slack exposed.” On Windows, press Win+Tab to create another desktop. On Mac, you can use Mission Control to add a separate desktop space. Then keep Slack on one desktop and your presentation materials on another.

How to use it:

  1. Create a second virtual desktop before the meeting.
  2. Move Slack to that desktop.
  3. Keep your slides, browser, or demo on the desktop you’ll share.
  4. Switch carefully only when you need to check messages privately.

This method is helpful if you want Slack available without cluttering the workspace you’re showing to everyone else. It can also reduce the mental load of constantly minimizing and reopening apps.

The tradeoff is that it depends on your own discipline. If you accidentally switch desktops while sharing your entire screen, Slack can still appear. It’s safer than leaving Slack front and center, but not as foolproof as blurring or sharing a single window. Best for people who multitask heavily and already use multiple desktops every day.

5. Close or minimize Slack before the meeting starts

This is the least fancy option, but it’s still one of the most reliable. If Slack does not need to play any role in the meeting, closing it removes the problem completely. No visible messages, no sidebar, no unread threads, no accidental channel-name reveal.

How to use it:

  1. Finish any urgent Slack replies before the call.
  2. Quit the Slack desktop app, close the Slack browser tab, or at least minimize the window.
  3. Start screen sharing only after confirming Slack is gone from view.

This works best for short presentations, interviews, webinars, and client walkthroughs where your attention should already be on the call. It’s also a good habit when you’re tired or rushed and don’t want one more thing to remember.

The downside is convenience. You lose instant access to chats during the meeting, and you may miss something time-sensitive until the presentation ends. Still, for pure risk reduction, “just close Slack” remains surprisingly effective.

6. Use a generic blur extension like Page Blur or BlurWeb

If you want one privacy tool that works across many websites, a generic blur extension can help. Tools like Page Blur and BlurWeb are designed to blur elements or selected areas on web pages, which means you can use them on Slack, dashboards, CRMs, docs, and other browser-based tools.

That flexibility is the main appeal. If your workday jumps between many tabs with sensitive information, a general-purpose blur tool may fit better than a site-specific extension.

How to use it:

  1. Install the extension you prefer.
  2. Open Slack Web or another site with sensitive content.
  3. Blur the visible areas manually before you present.
  4. Test the page once to make sure the blur stays where you expect.

The tradeoff is precision. Generic tools are not built specifically for Slack’s layout, so they may require more manual setup each time. If Slack’s interface changes or you switch channels, you may need to reapply the blur. They’re useful, but for people who screen share Slack often, a Slack-specific tool is usually faster and less fiddly.

FAQ

Does Privacy Blur for Slack work in the Slack desktop app?

No. Privacy Blur for Slack works on app.slack.com in Chrome. If you mainly use the desktop app, you can switch to Slack Web before a meeting or use one of the non-extension methods in this guide.

Is Slack Do Not Disturb enough on its own?

Usually no. Do Not Disturb is great for stopping new notification popups, but it does not hide the Slack content that’s already sitting on your screen. If Slack will remain visible during the meeting, pair DND with either Privacy Blur for Slack or a window-sharing strategy.

What’s the best setup for people who screen share every day?

If Slack needs to stay open, the most practical setup is Privacy Blur for Slack + Slack Do Not Disturb. That combination protects visible content and reduces the chance of a surprise popup at the same time.

The simplest way to avoid an awkward Slack moment

If you screen share often and still need Slack open, use the tool built for that exact problem. Install Privacy Blur for Slack and blur messages, names, channels, and media before your next call. It’s free forever, works entirely client-side, and takes less than a minute to set up.
